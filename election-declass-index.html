01

Vulnerabilities in Electronic Voting and Ballot-Counting Systems

8 documents · Open folder in Drive →

The thinnest section, and the one furthest from the speech's claims. Two of eight documents are the mainline IC assessments that the dissent argues against; two more are the dissent and the FBI's rebuttal of it. Nothing here supports vote manipulation, and two documents actively disclaim it.

CISA Election Report

NO STAMP6 pp13 July 2026

Open in Drive → CISA Election Report - FINAL.pdf

What it is

A technical summary of CISA's 2019–2024 election security work: source-code and binary analysis of election software conducted with Idaho National Laboratory under the Critical Product Evaluation program, penetration testing of state, local, tribal and territorial networks, and incident response. Findings are that election software contains the ordinary spectrum of vulnerabilities; that certification regimes block timely patching, with some regimes requiring no patches for months before an election; and that SLTT networks lack basic segmentation, with assessors gaining full network control within hours or days in multiple cases. Cites Halderman's ImageCast X barcode analysis from Curling v. Raffensperger. Mitigation recommendations: harmonize patch and certification rules, use human-readable paper ballots, and conduct post-election manual audits before certification.

Why it matters

Written for this release and unclassified, but it cuts against the speech rather than for it. Its recommendations — paper ballots, manual audits — are the mainstream election-security consensus, not a case for the claims Trump made. Most usefully, it declines to endorse the Puerto Rico work: it states that ODNI commissioned a forensic examination of Dominion devices from Mojave Research, and that while CISA reviewed the report, the agency had no access to the devices and could not perform its own examination. That is the agency holding the pen refusing to vouch for the administration's centerpiece hardware finding.

NICM: Vulnerabilities in US 2020 Election Infrastructure

NO STAMP5 pp15 January 2020

Open in Drive → NICM_V~1.PDF

What it is

National Intelligence Council memorandum on infrastructure vulnerabilities ahead of the 2020 election. Assesses that cyber operations against tabulation could delay results reporting and create public uncertainty but probably would not affect the integrity of certified results, because tabulated results are stored independently of the copies displayed on results websites. Notes risk-limiting audits, then required in 38 states. Cites the Pennsylvania Blue Ribbon Commission finding that transmission of preliminary results to public websites is vulnerable to man-in-the-middle attacks that would not alter the separately stored certified copies. Recommends physical and cyber hygiene, third-party vendor verification, public messaging, and private messaging to adversaries.

Why it matters

The most quotable document in the release, for reasons the White House cannot have intended. A full section is headed 'False Manipulation Narratives Could Undermine Public Confidence.' It warns that adversaries could make wholly fabricated claims — the example given is announcing they had compromised all US voting machines — which would be difficult, time-consuming, or impossible for the government to disprove; that much of the voting public knows little about election administration, which could let false narratives gain traction; and that a widely publicized compromise would undercut confidence even if nothing was manipulated. It recommends timely public messaging to make US officials the trusted source. Written in January 2020, it describes the 2026 event it was released to support.

NICA 2020-06885D: Foreign Threats to 2020 US Federal Elections

TRUMP · 3 JUL8 pp19 August 2020DUPLICATE

Open in Drive → NICA_ Foreign Threats to 2020 US election_19AUG2020 - DECLASS_REDACTED.pdf

What it is

The mainline National Intelligence Council assessment on foreign election threats, in a downgraded version. Judges that Russia, China, Iran and many non-state actors have the capability to compromise election infrastructure but would find it difficult to manipulate voting at scale without detection. On Russia: assesses it is using a range of measures primarily to denigrate Biden, directing or encouraging proxies to spread claims about him, with some Kremlin-linked actors seeking to boost Trump's candidacy on social media; names Derkach and Kilimnik among proxies advancing a Burisma corruption narrative, with the stated aim of defeating Biden and ensuring Trump's victory. On China: assesses Beijing prefers Trump be defeated and has stepped up critical public rhetoric, but did not intend to try to affect the election. Scope note explicitly states the paper makes no assessment about the impact of these efforts.

Why it matters

Released under Trump's own name, and it contains the Russia-preferred-Trump finding in plain language. Also supplies the baseline that every other China document in the release is measured against, and the reason the dissent exists at all. The scope note matters for anyone trying to read impact into it: the assessment declines to make one.

NICM 2020-09381: Making the Case That China Has Taken Some Steps to Influence the Presidential Election

TRUMP · 3 JUL7 pp16 October 2020

Open in Drive → NICM_ChinaStepsToInfluenceElection_16OCT2020_DECLASS_REDACTED.pdf

What it is

The minority view itself, written by the National Intelligence Officer for Cyber and the Director of Election Threat Analysis. Header reads 'ALTERNATIVE ANALYSIS.' Scope note states it offers an alternative perspective to the IC's assessment that China has not attempted to influence the outcome, and that it 'was provided to the IC for comment before publication but is not an IC-coordinated memorandum.' The authors assess Beijing took at least low-level, exploratory steps to undermine Trump's reelection chances through overt messaging, nascent online covert influence capability, diplomatic measures and economic leverage; state these probably did not include Beijing's most aggressive options; and record low-to-medium confidence in their own assessments. They state they agree with the large majority of the IC's judgments about China's calculus. The memo reproduces the mainline view alongside: that Beijing has not deployed influence efforts intended to change the outcome, prefers stability in the relationship, and wants to preserve the ability to repair relations afterward. It concedes that much of the available reporting would be consistent with either view.

Why it matters

This is the source Trump quoted. The White House released it with every disqualifying marking intact — the alternative-analysis banner, the not-IC-coordinated disclaimer, the low-to-medium confidence, the authors' own concession that the reporting is ambiguous and that they agree with most of the mainline. The gap between what the speech said this document shows and what the document says about itself is the story, and it is legible from the first page.

Email: ICA Comments re Minority View

WARRINGTON · 10 JUL2 pp30 December 2020

Open in Drive → EMAIL_ICA.CommentsReMinorityView_30DEC2020_DECLASS REDACTED.pdf

What it is

Email from Nikki Floris, then Deputy Assistant Director of the FBI's Counterintelligence Division, sent to the Director of Election Threat Analysis and copied to Tonya Ugoretz, commenting on the updated ICA draft. The FBI is fine with a minority-view textbox but insists its analysis meet the same analytic standards as the rest of the ICA — 'an absolute red line for the FBI' — and opposes putting the minority view in the Key Judgments, where without context and source descriptions it would be given parity with the ICA's assessments. Point by point: on the NIO's claim that China's leaders 'intended' to affect candidates, the IC has repeatedly stated on a body of intelligence that there is no evidence of such intention, and stating it that bluntly is 'extremely misleading.' On sourcing, minimizing source descriptions gives clandestine reporting of dubious credibility, poorly identified sourcing chains and minimal track records the same weight as SIGINT on identified senior Chinese diplomatic and intelligence figures. On the claim China 'may' have encouraged protests, there is no evidence in the reporting and the citations do not support it — 'a misrepresentation of the underlying reports.' On the dissent's collection-gaps argument, citing the absence of evidence is 'an absolute red line for us.' The forwarding note records that the FBI had previously said Director Wray signed off on the ICA text.

Why it matters

The single most damaging document in the release, and the administration published it. It is a named senior FBI official's contemporaneous, itemized finding that the analysis Trump built a primetime address on failed the IC's own tradecraft standards. Floris is on the record and reachable. Note also that this sits in Section 01 rather than with the China material in Section 02, which is either filing drift or a deliberate burial — either way, worth asking about.

Email: Everyone's favorite topic

TRUMP · 3 JUL1 p23 December 2021DUPLICATE

Open in Drive → EMAIL_Everyone's favorite topic_23DEC2021_DECLASS_REDACTED.pdf

What it is

Email from the National Intelligence Officer for Cyber — the minority view's author — a year after the ICA. He argues that a new IC report cites some of the same intelligence and logic used in 2020 to conclude that particular Chinese units were not engaged in election influence, and now uses it to argue that China IS influencing a (redacted) election. He notes the report attributes activity to 'the Chinese military' when in 2020 the IC said it did not know who it was, and characterizes as an election-influence unit the same unit he and a colleague said was influencing the 2020 US election. He asks on what logical, non-partisan basis the IC makes calls one way or the other, and says it should be highlighted for oversight.

Why it matters

The dissenter's strongest argument, in his own words: an inconsistency claim about how the IC applies attribution standards across cases. It is a real methodological complaint and shouldn't be waved off — but it is an argument about IC consistency, not evidence that China altered a US election, and it does not answer Floris's sourcing objections.

CIA WIRe: China — Cyber Activities Probably Prelude to Election Espionage

WARRINGTON · 10 JUL2 ppSummer 2020DUPLICATE

Open in Drive → CIA Wire Memo Summer 2020 DECLASS REDACTED.pdf

What it is

CIA World Intelligence Review item on Chinese cyber activity around the campaign. Reports that China is probing the presidential campaign to tailor collection on US-China policy positions, and that Chinese cyber actors have done so in every US presidential campaign since at least 2008. Since 2018, actors known in the private sector as APT31 have targeted personal email accounts of senior US leadership including officials in the Executive Office of the President, Congress and the federal judiciary. As of 20 May, APT31 sent spear-phishing emails with tracking links to Gmail accounts of campaign staffers; on 4 June, Google announced APT31 was targeting the campaign, and Google and the FBI briefed campaign officials, with Google stating publicly that the attempts were unsuccessful. Records that during the past year Chinese cyber actors collected US election-related information from voter databases, a polling data company, political and nonprofit organizations, fundraisers and campaign advisory organizations.

Why it matters

Documents real Chinese cyber espionage against the Biden campaign — and then states the conclusion the speech needed to avoid: the IC assesses that China does not currently intend to covertly interfere to try to sway the outcome of the election, although the activity could enable such operations if Beijing decided to. Note the target was Biden's campaign, not Trump's, and Google says the phishing failed. Another Warrington-track release that contradicts the Trump-track framing.

CIA Note: Venezuela's Electronic Voting Manipulation Capabilities, 2004–2020

RATCLIFFE · 1 JUL6 pp29 June 2026

Open in Drive → CIA Note - Venezuela Machines Intel Memo_29JUNE2026_DECLASS_REDACTED.pdf

What it is

A 2026 CIA retrospective summarizing selected 2004–2020 reporting on Venezuelan interest in manipulating electronic voting systems. Finds that Venezuelan officials developed sustained interest and likely some capability in manipulating electronic voting systems including Smartmatic technology; that the 2006 IC assessment that Smartmatic's Venezuela ties posed a national security threat rested on solid intelligence about Venezuelan intent to influence US politics and evidence of manipulation of Venezuela's own systems; and that this led the US to force Smartmatic to divest its US operations in 2007. Cites April 2004 reporting that Chávez stated an objective of preventing the reelection of a sitting US president.

Why it matters

Reads as a setup for a Dominion/Smartmatic revival and then refuses to deliver. Its own summary states the intelligence did not definitively confirm that large-scale electronic fraud was successfully executed in specific Venezuelan elections, that CIA's baseline assessments maintained other factors better explained electoral outcomes, and that reporting on the more advanced techniques came from limited sourcing. Everything concrete concerns Venezuela's domestic elections and a US divestment order completed nineteen years ago. Carries a distinct third declassification stamp — Ratcliffe, 1 July — with Warrington's release approval added 10 July.

China's Acquisition and Exploitation of American Voter Data

23 documents · Open folder in Drive →

The section carrying the speech's headline claim. Six documents are duplicates of Section 01 material. The operative finding — that the underlying data was publicly available and downloaded from commercial websites — appears in the government's own correction notice, filed here.

WHTF Government Transparency — States Statement

NO STAMP1 p13 July 2026

Open in Drive → WHTF Government Transparency - States Statement FINAL.pdf

What it is

The White House Government Transparency Task Force's cover statement. Records that Trump created the task force within the Executive Office of the President in May 2026 to advise him, through the Counsel to the President, on documents that should be declassified or released 'to further his administration's priorities.' Announces the first disclosure, states that declassified intelligence reveals voter registration rolls from at least 18 states have been compromised by the PRC, and that additional records reveal more than 200 million voter records were compromised 'without state-specific affiliations.' Names sixteen jurisdictions: Alaska, Arkansas, Colorado, Connecticut, District of Columbia, Florida, Georgia, Iowa, Kansas, Maryland, Michigan, New York, North Carolina, Ohio, Oklahoma, Rhode Island.

Why it matters

The document that turns intelligence reporting into a political claim, and the one to check the rest of the section against. Three problems on a single page. It says 'at least 18 states' and names sixteen jurisdictions, one of which (DC) is not a state. It concedes the 200 million figure has no state-specific affiliation, which means the headline number cannot be tied to any named system. And the task force's stated purpose is not transparency in the abstract but furthering the administration's priorities — the charter is in the text. Ohio's presence on this list is not supported by the document it derives from (see 'US Voter Registration for 6 States'), which records that the Ohio download failed.

US Voter Registration for 6 States — Network Defense Notice (Substantive Revision) KEY

NO STAMP6 ppJanuary 2022 activity; revision undated

Open in Drive → US Voter Registration for 6 States - clean - declass marked_Redacted.pdf

What it is

A network defense notice, issued as a substantive revision of an earlier report. Records that publicly available US voter registration information for six states — Colorado, Connecticut, Florida, Michigan, Oklahoma and Rhode Island — was downloaded by a PRC computer network exploitation actor on 14 January 2022, from US commercial websites and via a US IP address. Records that an Ohio voter registration application was also of interest to the actor but failed to download. The revision notice states that Oklahoma was added and that 'the websites hosting the voter registration records are commercial websites, and that fact has been clarified in the report,' directing recipients to remove the original report from all manual and computer files.

Why it matters

The load-bearing document for the entire 'compromise' framing, and it demolishes it in its own correction notice. The government issued a formal revision for the specific purpose of clarifying that this was publicly available data downloaded from commercial websites — that is, voter files bought or scraped from data brokers, which anyone can do, and which is not a breach of anything. It also dates the activity to January 2022, not the 2020 cycle. Two hard facts for Ohio reporting: the state is on the White House's compromised list, and the only Ohio entry in the underlying intelligence is a failed download of a registration application. The 'at least 18 states' claim rests substantially on this six-state notice.

200M Voter Records Compromised KEY

WARRINGTON · 10 JUL24 pp2019 source documentOCR PARTIAL

Open in Drive → 200M Voter Records Compromised - declass marked_Redacted.pdf

What it is

Heavily redacted report whose actual title is that the PRC 'possesses list of likely leaked, compromised data.' Describes a document held as of 2019 containing a list of entities and the data they yielded, primarily dated 2009–2018. A comparatively small portion — 97 entries — were explicitly identified as originating from US entities; the majority of the list consisted of data from other countries. The majority of entries were data sets of personally identifiable information, potentially indicating that bulk PII collection was the goal, and several were characterized as voter registration records. A table lists 'Unspecified U.S. voter data, 204,822,241 records (45 GB), 2016, includes voters' names, ages, phone numbers, addresses, etc.', plus named state databases of 11,746,069 records (2017), 7,893,248 records (2017) and 5,578,302 records (2017), the last with citizenship information.

Why it matters

The source of the '220 million voter files.' The document says 204.8 million. It dates the data to 2016 and the list to 2019 — the speech said the compromise began in the 2020 cycle. And its framing is an inventory of data already leaked or compromised and in circulation, not an account of a Chinese acquisition operation: possessing a list of breached data sets is not the same act as breaching them. The US material is a minority of a list that is mostly other countries. Redaction is heavy enough that OCR on several pages is unrecoverable; the table on pages 3–4 is the legible core and should be read directly.

OCR partial. Pages 2 and 5–24 are largely redacted or illegible; characterization above rests on pages 1, 3 and 4.

Summary — PART 1

WARRINGTON · 10 JUL1 pundated

Open in Drive → Summary - clean - declass marked PART 1.pdf

What it is

One-page summary asserting that ahead of the 2020 election China had extensive plans to use influence and cyber operations to sway opinion against the Trump administration, designed to exploit US societal fissures across several scenarios including US-China military conflict. Themes listed here: economic recession; encouraging violent demonstrations and looting to increase the appearance of social unrest; intensifying disapproval of the President and calling his ability to govern into question; undermining the legitimacy and public support of the President; ineffective USG COVID-19 response. States China had developed capabilities to project these themes into social media (TikTok, Facebook, Twitter, YouTube) and mainstream media through overt and hidden influencers, and that one option included gathering information on senior US officials.

Why it matters

Read the verbs. 'Had extensive plans,' 'had developed capabilities,' 'one of the options included' — this is a capabilities-and-options document, not a record of executed operations, and it contains no voter data at all despite being filed in the voter-data section. Parts 1, 2 and 3 share an identical opening paragraph and identical closing paragraph, differing only in the bulleted theme list, which suggests a single source product split into three releases rather than three findings.

Email: NSA deliberately massaging elections PDB (20 Nov) KEY

TRUMP · 3 JUL8 pp20 November 2020OCR PARTIAL

Open in Drive → EMAIL_NSA.MassagedPDB_20NOV2020_DECLASS_REDACT.pdf

What it is

Email chain within ODNI regarding the 45-day ICA China section drafting team. A sender states that there is pending reporting making clear China 'has been caught conducting election influence,' at least as it was described to them, and points to a claim upthread that NSA 'deliberately massaged our one pending PDB to avoid any direct links to the election.' The sender frames this as a near-term issue and as a good example — 'far from the only one' — of what they have been raising since the summer: that the IC is deliberately avoiding mentioning a connection to elections for non-substantive reasons. Why it matters

What it is

Why it matters

SAVE — the system that actually checks citizenship against USCIS records — returned about 28,000 non-citizens across 68 million records, a rate near 0.04 percent. The 250,000 claim across four states with roughly 30 million combined registrants implies about 0.8 percent, twenty times the verified rate. No methodology is given for the 250,000; the difference between a SAVE match and a flag generated by reviewing a public file is the entire question, and the document does not address it. Note also that this is the only document in the release that editorializes in its own text — 'activist Judge,' 'alien-first policies' — which tells you what kind of product it is.

Recognizing and Addressing Threats to Statewide Voter Registration Databases KEY

NO STAMP11 ppJuly 2026

Open in Drive → Voter Registration Database Threats - FINAL.pdf

What it is

An eleven-page unclassified overview of threats to statewide voter registration databases from foreign and domestic actors, drawing on IC, law enforcement and state election official reporting, intended to inform state and local election officials. Opens by asserting that recently declassified records revealed China breached multiple state voter registration systems prior to the 2020 election, that this is not new, that hackers have attempted to breach voter registration systems in all 50 states with confirmed successes in at least 20, and that experts have routinely minimized the significance of successful breaches. Argues for realistic assessment of threats and against minimizing risks, and calls for defenses to prevent stolen data being used to obtain absentee ballots, alter registration information, or add and delete registrants.

Why it matters

The circularity is on the first page. Its evidentiary premise is 'recently declassified records revealed that China breached multiple state voter registration systems' — that is, this release. So the task force's document cites the task force's release as proof of the claim the release was assembled to make, and then the underlying document in Section 02 turns out to say the data was publicly available on commercial websites. Everything downstream of that premise inherits it. Read as a threat assessment stripped of the premise, the mitigation advice is unobjectionable and largely matches CISA's; read as evidence, it is an assertion citing itself.

Method and limits

What was checked

The Drive folder holds 58 PDFs across four folders. Every one was verified by byte size against the original download; all uploaded intact, and all 55 distinct documents in the release are present. Three files carry truncated 8.3 filenames from the upload (NICM_V~1.PDF, PRCUSP~1.PDF, EMAIL_~3.PDF); their contents are unaffected and their real titles are given above. Two stray .DS_Store files sit in the Michigan and Noncitizens folders and can be deleted.

Counts and duplicates

The download contains 59 PDF file paths but only 55 distinct documents. Four are byte-identical duplicates, confirmed by checksum: the August 2020 NICA, the CIA WIRe memo and the December 2021 "Everyone's favorite topic" email are each filed in both Section 01 and Section 02, and the four-country graphic email is stored twice inside Section 02, once at the folder root and once in the numbered subfolder. Drive holds 58 because that fourth redundant copy did not survive the upload — nothing distinct is missing. Both members of each cross-section pair are indexed above, since the filing itself is evidence of how the release was assembled.

Text recovery

Nearly every document is an image-only scan whose embedded text layer contains only the declassification stamp. All 59 were rasterised at 200 dpi and read by OCR. Entries tagged OCR PARTIAL are ones where redaction or scan quality left material unrecoverable; for those, the summary reflects only what is legible, and the "why it matters" note says so. Where a filename asserts something the released text does not support — which is common in Section 03 — that gap is flagged rather than papered over.

What this index does not do

It does not adjudicate the underlying intelligence questions, which would require the withheld material: the ICA drafts, the PDB at issue in the November 2020 chain, the Floris–Ugoretz communications log, the IIR recall statistics from 2019 to 2025, and the Person_Checks_2023.xlsx spreadsheet. Each of those is referenced in a released document and absent from the release.
